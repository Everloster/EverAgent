# Sleep Science: SWS, GH, Training Recovery

## SWS-GH Coupling (Born, Muth & Fehm, 1988)
**Landmark finding** (Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism):
- **~70% of nocturnal GH secretion** occurs during slow-wave sleep (N3/SWS)
- GH pulses are **time-locked to SWS onset**—deeper/longer SWS = stronger GH burst
- Delayed sleep onset → first SWS phase delayed → first GH pulse compressed
- Total night sleep preservation ≠ GH preservation if SWS architecture is disrupted

### Mechanism
SWS is the **only sleep stage** permitting large GH secretion pulses. Fragmented entry into SWS (e.g., high cortisol, elevated body temp post-exercise) delays and dampens the first pulse—critical for post-training anabolism.

## Sleep Deprivation & Testosterone
Matthew Walker's meta-analysis findings:
- **Single night <6h sleep** → testosterone ↓ 10–15%
- **5 consecutive nights at 5h/night** → testosterone decrement equivalent to **10–15 years of aging**
- Mechanism: Elevated cortisol (catabolism) suppresses LH → reduced testosterone synthesis

### Critical Mismatch
**Scenario**: 90 min strength training (high catabolic state) → only 60 min buffer → difficult sleep onset
- Result: Reduced SWS entry + elevated cortisol overnight
- Net metabolic outcome: Positive protein balance unlikely; training effect attenuated

## Post-Training Sleep Dose-Response (Nature Communications, 2025)
**14,000+ wearable device dataset** quantifying evening exercise → sleep impact:

| Time gap (exercise end to bed) | Sleep latency (min) | REM % change | Deep sleep % change |
|--------------------------------|--------------------|--------------|--------------------|
| ≥4 hours | No change | No change | No change |
| 2–3 hours | +5–10 | −10–15% | −5% |
| 1–2 hours (high intensity) | +15–25 | −20–30% | −10–15% |
| <1 hour | +25–45 | −30–50% | −15–25% |

**Implication**: <1h buffer (your scenario at 60 min post-training) = high-risk zone for SWS compression and GH attenuation.

## GH Replacement Cannot Reverse Sleep-Driven Anabolism Loss
GH is necessary but insufficient for post-exercise recovery without SWS. Even if total GH is preserved through medication, the **pulsatile, sleep-entrained secretion pattern** drives muscle protein synthesis more efficiently than constant infusion.
