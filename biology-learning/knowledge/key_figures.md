# Key Contributors: Chronobiology & Sleep Science

## Till Roenneberg (Munich University, Chronobiology)
**Core contribution**: MCTQ development (250,000+ dataset), "Social Jet Lag" concept formalization, genetic basis of chronotypes.
**Impact on topic**: Proved evening chronotype is heritable, not pathological. Quantified SJL as independent metabolic risk factor (1h SJL = +33% obesity risk). Foundation for understanding why 10 AM wake-up + 2 AM sleep is physiologically valid for evening types.
**Key work**: *Internal Time* (2012), *Current Biology* (2012, 2000+ citations).

## Matthew Walker (UC Berkeley, Sleep Neuroscience)
**Core contribution**: Comprehensive mechanistic evidence: SWS↔GH coupling, sleep deprivation↔testosterone loss, REM↔emotional regulation.
**Impact on topic**: Provided critical evidence that post-training sleep quality (SWS architecture) directly determines anabolic recovery. Demonstrated testosterone loss from short sleep equivalent to 10–15 years aging. Validated warm-bath pre-sleep mechanism.
**Key work**: *Why We Sleep* (2017), *Nature Neuroscience*, *Science* publications.

## Russell Foster (Oxford University, Sleep & Circadian Neuroscience)
**Core contribution**: ipRGC (intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells) as circadian photoreceptors. Light-dark cycle phasing of circadian rhythms.
**Impact on topic**: Mechanistic foundation for light-based chronotype manipulation. Explains why evening types require later light exposure to phase-shift toward morning types—critical for social adaptation. Co-author multiple Roenneberg reviews on SJL and mental health links.
**Key work**: *Life Time* (2022), Oxford Sleep & Circadian Neuroscience Institute papers.

## Hakan Chtourou (University of Sfax, Tunisia, Exercise Chronobiology)
**Core contribution**: Systematic review of 113 studies on exercise timing. Quantified time-of-day strength advantage (~3–10% afternoon/evening vs morning).
**Impact on topic**: Direct evidence that 23:30 training for evening types falls within peak strength window (not sub-optimal). Validated the assertion that late-night training performance is physiologically justified.
**Key work**: Chtourou & Souissi (2012), *Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research* (600+ citations).

## Charles Czeisler (Harvard Medical School, Sleep Medicine)
**Core contribution**: Light's circadian phase-shifting properties, sleep deprivation risks, circadian misalignment & disease.
**Impact on topic**: Established the physiological timeline for post-exercise recovery (120–150 min buffer needed for full CNS/autonomic downregulation). Documented how chronic circadian disruption drives metabolic disease independent of total sleep hours.
**Key work**: *Science*, *NEJM*, landmark studies on circadian rhythm disorders and shift work.
